Case Study · Medicare Claims Operations

Giving MACs the rules they're left to write.

National Medicare policy from CMS can't anticipate every local claim scenario — so Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs) fill the gap with localized business rules. They were doing it on legacy, mainframe-based tools that made those rules hard to see, slow to change, and easy to get wrong. I designed a clearer way for MACs to create and manage the rules they own.

01 Overview

The rules governing local Medicare claims lived in a mainframe almost no one could read.

CMS sets national Medicare policy, but that policy can't anticipate every local claim scenario. MACs — the regional contractors who process Medicare claims — fill those gaps with localized business rules that determine how specific claims are handled.

Those rules were authored and maintained in legacy, mainframe-based tools. The logic was hard to see, slow to change, and easy to get wrong, with little transparency into what a rule actually did or why.

The goal: give MACs a clearer, faster, and more transparent way to create and manage the localized rules they're responsible for.

› Current-State Process

Implementing a single rule change today runs through five roles and takes 30–90 days.

Mapping the existing BPMN process made the cost of the legacy tooling concrete: a policy change request hands off from CMS to business analysts, coders, testers, and operations — with a test-fail loop back to revision — before rules ever reach production. Every handoff is a place for delay, rework, and lost context.

03 Users & Audience

Built for the MAC staff who own the rules — and faithful to the precision Medicare claims demand.

The tool had to serve people with deep claims and policy expertise but no engineering background, while still producing logic precise enough to govern real Medicare claims correctly.

Primary
MAC rule authors
Medicare Administrative Contractor staff who create and maintain localized business rules. Deep claims and policy expertise; underserved by legacy mainframe tooling.
Secondary

Stakeholders
CMS & program owners

Affected
Providers & beneficiaries
Never see the tool, but experience its output: how their Medicare claims are evaluated, paid, or denied.
04 Roles & Responsibilities

I translated dense conditional logic into an interface a non-engineer could reason about.

I led research with MAC rule authors and the engineers who maintained the existing tools, then designed the information architecture and interaction model for the rule builder — from how a single condition reads to how nested AND/OR logic stays comprehensible as it grows.

The core challenge was cognitive: making branching logic feel structured rather than tangled. I used Human Factors principles to externalize state, preview consequences before publishing, and prevent the class of errors that used to reach production.

05 Scope & Constraints

06 Process & What I Did

A multi-method research effort — system analysis, stakeholder interviews, and collaborative workshops — to teach current workflows to engineers and explore what a modern system could be.

I led the effort end to end, combining system analysis, stakeholder interviews, and collaborative workshops to surface how MAC rule work actually happens — then carried that understanding forward to explore solution pathways for a modern business-rules system.

07 Outcomes & Lessons

The work delivered a set of viable, side-by-side-evaluated pathways to modernize MAC-controlled business rules.

Rather than a single recommendation, the research produced a decision framework: three solution options assessed against shared evaluation criteria, with the trade-offs of each made explicit so CMS and MAC stakeholders could make an informed build-, integrate-, or buy-decision.

Custom-Built MAP Capability Mainframe Integration Third-Party Service
PROS
  • Tailored specifically to CMS/MAC operational requirements
  • Seamless integration with existing MAC infrastructure and workflows
  • Long-term flexibility for future policy changes and enhancements
  • Complete control over features, security, and compliance measures
  • Leverages existing mainframe infrastructure and investments
  • Faster initial deployment using established system architecture
  • Lower short-term implementation costs and resource requirements
  • Familiar technology stack for existing development teams
  • Potentially faster implementation with pre-built solutions
  • Modern UI/UX design patterns and user-experience standards
  • Access to specialized vendor expertise and domain knowledge
  • Reduced internal development and maintenance overhead
CONS
  • Higher upfront development costs and resource allocation
  • Longer development timeline before system deployment
  • Ongoing maintenance burden and technical-support requirements
  • Risk of scope creep and implementation delays
  • Limited scalability and performance constraints of legacy systems
  • Continued reliance on aging mainframe infrastructure
  • Difficult to modernize and adapt for future technological changes
  • Limited user experience and interface modernization options
  • Vendor lock-in risks and dependency on external provider
  • Complex integration challenges with existing CMS/MAC systems
  • Potential compliance and security risks with external data handling
  • Limited customization options for CMS-specific requirements

› Lessons
→

The tooling was the constraint, not the expertise. MACs already knew the rules — the work was giving them a transparent place to express them.

→

Modernization is a trade-off space, not a single answer. The most useful outcome was framing the options against shared criteria, not forcing one verdict.

→

Bringing engineers into the research paid off. Workshops that taught current workflows built the shared understanding every option depended on.
