Case Study · Flexion · 2022–2024

Modernizing Medicare business rules for the first time in decades

Medicare Advantage Contractors (MACs) fill the gap between CMS federal policy and real-world claims. They manage those rules in mainframe-bound, high-friction legacy systems. I led a multi-method research engagement to map the current state and produce three viable modernization pathways.

01 — Problem

CMS policy doesn't cover everything. MACs fill the gap. Legacy tools make that dangerous.

When federal Medicare policy doesn't address a specific claims scenario, Medicare Advantage Contractors write local business rules to handle it. These rules are created, edited, and audited in mainframe-based systems that lack transparency, slow down policy response, and create error risk at scale.

MACs need a better way to create and manage localized business rules that CMS policy doesn't cover.

Understand current MAC workflows, identify constraints, and explore modernization opportunities that maintain compliance.

Stakeholder report with current state analysis and three validated pathways for modernization.

03 — Methods

Three methods. All primary data.

Stakeholder Interviews

First-hand perspectives gathered from MAC analysts, coders, testers, and CMS stakeholders. I used consistent questions across roles to enable comparison and avoid confirmation bias.

Collaborative Workshops

Structured sessions for solution generation. Breakout groups, gallery walls, and dot voting with criteria: feasibility, alignment with CMS goals, and creative potential.

System Analysis

Reviewed MAP platform capabilities, mainframe infrastructure, and third-party integration points to understand what was technically possible — not just what was desirable.

"I led this research to teach current workflows to engineers — and to find pathways forward that they hadn't been able to see from inside the system."
04 — Workshop Design

A structured ideation method that produced consensus, not just ideas

I designed and facilitated a multi-stage workshop. The problem was shared with all participants before any ideation began — a deliberate choice to prevent anchoring on existing assumptions.

1

Breakout Groups

Six groups worked independently within a colored workspace. Each created 3 concept stickies, then selected one to advance — forcing prioritization before sharing.

2

Gallery Wall

Selected ideas from all groups were collected in a shared space, visible to everyone. This created a collective view of the solution space before any evaluation began.

3

Dot Voting

Participants voted with red and blue dots. Criteria: feasibility within MAC systems, alignment with CMS and MAC goals, and creative approach to working differently.

Why this structure matters: Without a shared problem statement at the start, groups anchor on different assumptions. Forcing each group to select one idea before the gallery prevents the loudest voice from dominating synthesis. These are Human Factors facilitation principles, not just design workshop conventions.
05 — Current State

Layered If/Then logic. Mainframe-bound. No audit trail.

What the system analysis revealed

The Medicare Claims System uses layered conditional logic across multiple subsystems. Coders, Analysts, and Testers each manage different parts of the rules lifecycle — but in a high-friction, mainframe-bound process with no unified view.

Three user roles touch the business rules system at different stages, with handoffs that introduced delay, inconsistency, and error risk at each transition point.

Key finding: The current system architecture made it impossible to see the full impact of a rule change before it was applied. This is a Human Factors error-induction problem — the system design forced risky decisions under uncertainty.

Three roles in the business rules lifecycle

Coder

Writes and edits business rules in the mainframe environment

Analyst

Interprets CMS policy and translates into rule requirements

Tester

Validates rule logic before deployment to claims processing

07 — Solution Options

Three pathways to modernization — evaluated, not just listed

I did not recommend one solution. I presented three options with a structured evaluation matrix across feasibility, alignment, and user impact — and let stakeholders make an informed decision with the data in hand.

01

Incremental modernization within existing MAP infrastructure

Lowest technical risk. Reduces friction without retiring the mainframe. Highest continuity for existing workflows.

02

Third-party rules engine integration

Replaces mainframe rule logic with a dedicated business rules management system. Higher capability, higher integration complexity.

03

Full re-architecture with modern rules platform

Highest long-term value. Requires technical feasibility study and phased implementation plan before committing.

08 — Outcome

What this engagement delivered — and what it didn't

What was delivered

CMS received a stakeholder report with current state analysis, a system architecture diagram, and three structured modernization options — each evaluated against feasibility, alignment, and user impact criteria.

The research surfaced a Human Factors problem that had been invisible to the technical team: the mainframe architecture forced high-stakes decisions under uncertainty, with no way to preview the impact of a rule change before applying it. That finding reframed the modernization goal from "retire legacy technology" to "reduce decision risk for analysts."

Honest scope note: This engagement ended at the research and strategy phase.
