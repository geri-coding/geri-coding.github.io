01 — Problem

No shared direction. No UX process. No research foundation.

A 7-engineer team was assigned to deliver a Design System proof of concept for Meridian, an Internal Developer Platform built to support software engineers in accessing, managing, and building applications across the organization. The team could not align on a direction.

Without a shared understanding of what software engineers needed to accomplish, design decisions were being driven by opinion rather than evidence. The risk was not aesthetic. An internal developer platform that does not match how engineers think about their work will not be adopted — regardless of how well it is built.

The Human Factors problem was to establish what engineers needed, make that knowledge visible to the team, and test design concepts against it before committing to a direction.

Context: As a contractor, I was excluded from team meetings by default. All research access was obtained proactively through PM-facilitated sessions and direct recruitment.
02 — Users and Context

High cognitive load. Time-constrained. Spread across too many tools.

Primary users: Software Engineers

Confirmed through two facilitated alignment workshops. The team reached consensus early: the platform should solve for the developer persona only. Non-developer needs were explicitly scoped out as an anti-goal.

Engineers operated across multiple external tools with no single view of their owned systems, team health, or active incidents. Their primary tasks included deploying applications, managing secrets, monitoring service status, reviewing pull requests, and resolving vulnerabilities.

Tools in use: GitLab, Dynatrace, SNYK, ServiceNow, and Argo CD — none integrated into one view.

Engineer Tasks

Deploy and manage applications
Monitor service status and vulnerabilities
Review and merge pull requests
Manage secrets and credentials
Resolve incidents and open vulnerabilities

04 — Discovery

Twelve pain points. Three independent sources. One triangulated brief.

Naturalistic observation

I attended PM-facilitated group feedback sessions and captured engineer pain points verbatim as they surfaced in natural conversation. I did not prompt or lead. The goal was to understand the work as engineers described it — not as a product team interpreted it.

Twelve distinct pain points were identified and mapped to Nielsen Norman Group heuristics. These became the validation criteria for every design decision that followed.

Facilitated alignment workshops

I designed and facilitated two 60-minute affinity mapping sessions with all 7 engineers. In Session 1, engineers generated sticky notes, shared them aloud, clustered by theme, dot-voted to surface priorities, and discussed only the top-voted clusters. I manually transcribed every sticky note in real time — no participant had FigJam access.

Session 2 was more challenging. The team was less cooperative. I maintained focus and closed decisions on all seven questions. Final consensus was reached.

Key output from workshops: A central platform to access, view, and manage owned workspaces. Data requirements — what engineers need to see in a workspace — became the anchor for all subsequent design work.

05 — Design

Three concepts. Each directly traceable to research.

Three homepage concepts were designed in blueprint wireframe style. Every card, every navigation item, and every interaction pattern was mapped to a specific pain point or workshop output. Cortex IDP was analyzed as a competitive reference and directly informed Concept 1.

Concept 1 — Cortex-Informed

Full Feature Set

  • Card-based homepage
  • My Work (replaces banner)
  • My On-calls (service down)
  • My Workspaces
  • Top Score cards (test results)
  • Secrets Navigator card + full page
  • Search modal with tag filters
Concept 2 — Paired Down

Navigation Simplification

  • Top mega menu minimized
  • Left nav: 11 items → 7 (36% reduction)
  • Named labels retained
  • Personalized greeting
  • My Work, Vulnerabilities, Service Status
  • Node Builder — speculative concept
Concept 3 — Workspace-Centered

Tabbed Interface

  • 3 tabs: My Workspace, Team Workspace, Workspace Data
  • Team tab: members, metrics, ownership, APIs
  • Workspace Data tab intentionally empty
  • Directly driven by Q1 workshop output
  • Research probe — not a gap
Design tension resolved in Concept 2: Pain point #9 said less information. Pain point #7 said show new things. Reducing the navigation by 36% addressed clutter while the My Work card surfaced timely updates — two competing needs resolved in one layout.
06 — Formative Evaluation

What engineers actually said.

Five engineers were recruited directly — I identified the most friction-aware participants from PM-facilitated sessions, avoiding management-mediated invitation. The PM was also included. Each participant viewed all three concepts via individual Figma prototype links on their own devices. Sessions were not recorded per enterprise security protocol and participant confidentiality. Findings are based on contemporaneous field notes.

Method: Concept testing — 3 concepts, 6 questions each. Questions covered navigation mental model, expectation gaps, feature relevance, task alignment, IA mental model, and efficiency perception.
"Nothing in here is my work." — Engineer, responding to Concept 1's My Work card · Identified a label recognition problem, not a scope problem

This was the most significant finding in Concept 1. The term "My Work" did not map to how engineers understand their own tasks, which live in GitLab and ServiceNow. The issue was not the concept — it was the label.

"I would never use the top menu. Never." — Engineer, responding to Concept 2's mega menu · Validated the decision to minimize it
"Cool feature. This would make work more efficient." — Engineer, responding to Concept 3's Team Workspace tab · Surfaced a need not in the original 12 pain points

The Team Workspace tab surfaced an unmet need that had not appeared in the original 12 pain points: knowing which teammates are working or out of office, and having early warning of active incidents before opening email.

"Usually this is always manually for us. AWS connections we are connecting manually. LeanIX. This is a good opportunity." — Engineer, responding to Node Builder speculative concept · Confirmed a real pain point without validating the specific solution

07 — Outcome

The first structured research practice on this team.

Findings from concept testing were presented to the team. Some findings were validated by the team. Design iteration was initiated based on participant feedback. Three versions of a workspace onboarding prototype were designed and delivered in collaboration with an engineer, applying H1 System Status, H8 Minimalist Design, and H9 Error Recovery across a stepped progression flow.

This was the first time a UX and Human Factors practitioner had embedded structured research methods into this team's workflow — establishing a repeatable foundation for future design decisions.
