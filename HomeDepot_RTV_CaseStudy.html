Replacing four legacy systems with one mobile application

01 — Problem

Five problems. Four applications. One broken process.

When I joined the RTV team, there were five documented pain points that formed the foundation of the design brief. I did not accept these at face value — I used them as hypotheses to investigate through research.

Too much associate decision-making — routing logic lived in associates' heads, not the system

Labor wasted recovering margin — dispositions were missed or incorrect

Preparing shipments was too complicated — required navigating multiple screens across systems

Too many separate worklists — associates context-switched between four applications

Need to retire green screens — legacy COBOL terminals were slow and error-prone

03 — Discovery

I went to the store before I opened Figma

Contextual inquiry in Atlanta

I arranged visits to a Home Depot warehouse in the greater Atlanta area and observed three associate roles as they processed returns in real time: the Customer Service Associate who receives the item, the Runner who moves it to the back, and the Receiving Associate who dispositions it.

I brought a notebook and my iPhone. I used identical questions across all participants. I informed associates that their participation was voluntary and would be anonymized.

Finding: The RTV process crossed three separate employee roles, four applications, and two store locations — a complexity invisible from the stakeholder brief.

Five phases emerged from the process map

I studied archival process documentation and identified a pattern. What had been described as a single "returns process" was actually five distinct phases, each with its own decision logic and error modes.

5-Phase RTV Model

1 — Customer Return Intake
2 — RTV Determination
3 — Physical Product Movement
4 — Verification
5 — Disposition

05 — Design Decision

From green screens to one scannable mobile app

Associates navigated between a COBOL green-screen terminal and multiple desktop applications to process a single return. The design goal was to eliminate that context-switching entirely — scan the item once, let the system route it.

After — SKU Mover

One app. Scan. Done.

  • Single mobile application
  • System determines routing automatically
  • Material Design components
  • Unified worklist
  • Guided disposition flow
  • 55% faster task completion
Validation method: I captured side-by-side screen recordings of the old process vs. the new prototype and presented them to stakeholders at sprint reviews. The visual comparison made the efficiency gain immediately legible without requiring any data interpretation.
06 — Delivery

Research in stores. Tested in stores.

I did not test at a desk. I wrote a usability script, made store appointments, and tested the Axure prototype on my personal phone — held in the associate's hand, the way it would actually be used. I also ran remote sessions via Zoom for stores outside Atlanta.

Key learning from usability testing: Presenting the prototype on a physical device changed how associates interacted with it. Testing on a desktop screen underrepresented the scanning and one-handed interaction patterns that mattered most in the warehouse environment.
07 — Outcome

Built, deployed, and measurably faster

The application was built and implemented on the First Phone across Home Depot stores. All five phases of the redesigned process were handed to developers via agile user stories. Associates reported processing returns more efficiently, and the time-on-task reduction was validated.
